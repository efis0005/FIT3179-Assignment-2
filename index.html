EDUCATION IN VICTORIA.      

"How does Victoria hold up when it comes to education? Lets have a look at our performance in non-tertiary schooling..."

This interactive chlorpleth map of Victorian Local Government Areas (LGAs) shows percentage of people who have graduated grade 10 and above

This graph shows the tie between location and education attainment for different regions in the state. Notably it appears the innermost suburbs closest to metropolotan Melbourne achieve the highest level of education attainment on average, with suburbs such as Melbourne, Port Phillip and Boroondara seeing ~95% of their inhabitants graduating grade 10 at minimum.

The outer suburbs fare the worst with LGAs such as Loddon, Hindsmarsh and Central Goldfields seeing only ~70% of their inhabitants graduating grade 10 or better.


The data is clear- urban environments are educated environments.

This shows the total Victorian students who continue their schooling into the next year, comparing rates for Government Schools and Independent Schools .

We can note that independent schools perform best at retaining students by a fairly large margin, typically retaining ~%5 more students on average when compared to the next best school type ( Non-government schools ).

Government Schools on the other hand are unfortunately quite poor at getting their students to return, especially in recent years, where only approximately 85% of students return year on year.

This map of Victoria shows the location of all primary, secondary and combined schools, colour coding based on if they are a Government School, Independent School, or Catholic School.

Use the zoom slider and drop down menu to interactively get a better idea of school density all accross the State. From an overview it is clear that schools are densely distributed around the CBD and get more and more sparse further from the city.

Population centres such as Bendigo and Shepparton see schools densely distributed near the centre of their suburbs; while coastal cities such as Sale and Geelong typically see their schools constructed quite close to the beach.

EDUCATION IN AUSTRALIA.      

How about on a National Level? Does Australias stellar reputation for tertiary level education reflect reality?

This national chloropleth map shows how good each state is at tertiary enrolments relative to the national average

This graph was created by deriving the percentage of citizens per state enrolled in post-secondary (tertiary) education and comparing it to the national average to display which states have high populations of students relative to the overall population and other states.

Notably we can see that South Australia and Tasmania have the least amount of its citizens enrolled in tertiary education , with their rates being quite signifcantly below the national average (typically ~5-8% below). This is fairly inline with expectation, as most highly rated Australian universities are in Queensland, New South Wales and Victoria.

This grouped bar chart demonstrates degree relevancy based on current role, comparing between Bachelor Degrees, Bachelor Degrees and Better, Qualifications below Bachlor Degrees and Qualifications totally irrelevant to field

Notably we can see the highest rates of relevancy between Qualification level and current role are for industries such as Technicians/Trade workers and Community and personal service workers. This is a fairly logical discovery as such roles typically require workers to become qualified ad hoc (ie. discover the role first then get qualified).

This is in quite stark contrast with most Bachelor Degree holders, who in almost all roles, rated their degree as approximately ~50% or lower relevant to their current role, Machine Operators and Drivers in particular seeing exceedingly low relevancy between role and bachelors degree ( only ~5% ).

EDUCATION IN THE WORLD.

Finally, Where is Australias place in the World when it comes to education? How does it fare with primary & secondary schooling and tertiary enrolment compared to other countries?

This global map shows the percentage of students enrolled in tertiary study, by country and year

The Year Interactivity Slider can be used to see the change in tertiary enrolments over a ~50 year period. It is particularly notable that true early adoption of tertiary education appeared to occur in Canada, the United States and Russia in the 80s.

Australia did not see widespread adoption of tertiary education (which it now has a repuation for) until approximately the mid 90s and has since steadily grown.

This bar chart displays the percentage of primary school age kids who are not enrolled in primary school, by region of the world

Whether children are enrolled in school by primary school age is a fairly good metric for measuring the attitudes of school adoption, and allow us to see how these attitudes differ according to location in the world.
There is also a disparity between income levels, with High and Middle Income countries seeing approximately 5%~10% of primary school age kids unerolled, while Low income countries see nearly %20 on average unerolled.
